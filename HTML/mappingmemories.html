Mapping Memory

Spatial Exploration of a Temporary Place

How do we remember what is not there? Remembering and preserving histories of protest is an ongoing act of resistance. When Resurrection City was deconstructed, the legacy of the Poor People's Campaign carried on, but no longer in the physical embodiment of a functioning city.

Use the digital map and archival photo slider below to reflect upon the lasting impact of Resurrection City and how knowing its history can impact your understanding on the present-day cultural landscape.

CITY HALL

City Hall was an important base of operations: a centrally-located, well-constructed shelter with electricity and phone access where residents could organize for daily marches and lobbying efforts. Although much of the SCLC senior staff, including Abernathy, lived offsite at the nearby Pitt Motel, they spent most of their time onsite in City Hall.

Reflection Prompt:

City Hall represents a specific structure within Resurrection City which speaks to the site's broader use as a gathering space for civil rights. What's in a building? What can a place of civic decision-making feel like when designed for citizens, by citizens - even if just a temporary structure?

SOLIDARITY DAY

On June 19th, 1968, over 50,000 people gathered in solidarity for working-class rights, culminating in a mass demonstration with songs, speakers, and prayer. This event was the peak of Resurrection City, helping promote the causes behind the movement and bring together civil rights activists and neighbors from across the country. A few days later, police flooded the settlement with tear gas, commencing the abandonment of the area and eventual deconstruction of the encampment.

Reflection Prompt:

Solidarity Day represents moment in space and time that highlights the culmination of labor, organizing, and visibility of Resurrection City within the built environment. The National Mall has been host to many protests, like Martin Luther King's March on Washington in 1963. What made Solidarity Day different? How did the settlement of Resurrection City create momentum for public demonstration and community organizing?

How to preserve a cultural landscape that has been lost or erased - or was meant to be temporary?

The Resurrection City Digital Atlas explores the possibilities for preserving cultural landscapes that have gone missing - whether erased, destroyed, overwritten, or ephemeral. The cultural landscape at the center of this project - Resurrection City - was a protest encampment located on the National Mall in 1968 by the Poor People's Campaign. Envisioned in 1967 by Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr., and fellow SCLC leaders, Resurrection City (RC) was built in May-June 1968 after King's assassination. The encampment marked an expression of the multiracial, anti-poverty, civil rights campaign that extended King's activism. This remarkable temporary city for nearly 3,000 inhabitants was built by activists and volunteers (with National Park Service permission) in the heart of monumental Washington. RC was a civil rights protest of extraordinary significance. It was dismantled in the summer of 1968, and today, no traces remain.

Resurrection City occupied the National Mall for two months in 1968 - an incredibly fraught moment on the most iconic memorial landscape in the US. In a democratic society, protests are an important demonstration that the political system is working, that dissent has a place. But how are protests preserved? Through stories, in history texts, perhaps on film - but rarely in the landscape itself, apart from a marker or a monument.

This "digital atlas" project explores means of cultural landscape documentation and interpretation for places significant to the civil rights history, particularly those poorly represented by traditional graphic documentation and historic preservation: places of protest, places deliberately erased, and places carelessly lost.

Responding to the paradoxical challenge of preserving an ephemeral landscape, we assembled images, public media, and archives - connecting them through mapping and apps - to produce a “digital atlas” re-presenting the spatial experiences of RC in conversation with the same spaces we experience today. It is a simulacrum of sorts, and a provocation to relate the erased and the actual landscape in our own moment.

The use of geographic methods is intended to make this work relatable and useful to others: the atlas functions as an interpretation of this particular site on the National Mall; as a framework for connecting to other adjacent civil rights landscapes in the DC monumental core; and as a prompt for others to build more and better digital atlases as a novel preservation workflow to meet future needs and priorities.

The Resurrection City Digital Atlas was produced by a dedicated and creative team of researchers and developers. Elizabeth Donison created a foundation of archival research; Daniel Watts led digital design and development work; Emily Holloway curated and crafted the whole digital atlas; Lia Schiffito provided development support on the atlas's participatory functions; and Randy Mason and Brent Fortenberry conceived the project with former NPS landscape architect Julie McGilvray. The project also benefited greatly from the historical expertise and insights of Kate Masur, Derek Musgrave, and Sarah Schoenfeld, as well as Cheryl LaRoche and Thaïsa Way.
